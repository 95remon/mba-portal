Consumer Behavior, Demand Estimation, and Elasticity

Consumer Behavior Theory

Introduction

The Consumer Problem

Key Points:

  • Profit and Consumer: Profit requires sales, and sales depend entirely on consumers. Thus, understanding consumer behavior is the cornerstone of profitable managerial decisions.
  • Consumer Optimization Problem:
    • Goal: Maximize satisfaction (or utility) from consumption.
    • Constraint: This maximization is subject to a budget constraint, where spending on goods must equal income.

Utility and Indifference Curves

Utility:

  • Economists use the concept of "Utility" to rank consumer preferences.
  • It expresses the benefits or satisfaction a consumer gets from consuming goods and services.

Indifference Curves:

  • A set of points representing different combinations of goods that all give the same level of utility.
  • On this curve, the consumer is "Indifferent" between any of these choices because they all satisfy them equally.

Shape of Indifference Curves

Characteristics:

  • Negatively Sloped: Slopes down from left to right. Meaning getting more of one good requires giving up some of the other.
  • Convex to Origin: Due to the diminishing marginal rate of substitution.
  • Multiple Curves: A higher curve represents a higher level of utility.

Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS)

Definition:

  • The rate at which one good is substituted for another while maintaining the same level of utility.
  • Equals the absolute value of the slope of the indifference curve.
  • It diminishes as we move down the indifference curve.
  • You are willing to give up a lot of Y to get one unit of X when you have very little X.

Formula: $$MRS = - \frac{\Delta Y}{\Delta X} = \frac{MU_X}{MU_Y}$$

Marginal Utility (MU)

Definition:

  • The addition to Total Utility resulting from consuming one extra unit of the good.
  • Diminishing Marginal Utility: As consumption of a good increases, the utility we get from each extra unit decreases.

Formula: $$MU = \frac{\Delta U}{\Delta X}$$

Consumer Budget Line

Definition:

  • Shows the feasible bundles for purchase given specific prices and fixed income.

Formula: $$M = P_X X + P_Y Y$$ Or in slope-intercept form: $$Y = \frac{M}{P_Y} - \frac{P_X}{P_Y} X$$

Budget Line Shifts

Types of Shifts:

  1. Changes in Income: Parallel shift of the budget line (Panel A). Increase in income shifts it out, decrease shifts it in.
  2. Changes in Price of X: Rotation around the Y-intercept (Panel B). Decrease in price of X makes it flatter (allows buying more X).

Utility Maximization

Concept:

  • The consumer seeks to reach the highest possible indifference curve within the limits of the budget constraint.
  • This happens at the point where the indifference curve is Tangent to the budget line.

Utility Maximization Condition

At the Tangency Point:

  • Slope of Indifference Curve (MRS) equals slope of Budget Line ($P_X/P_Y$).

  • Formula: $$MRS = \frac{MU_X}{MU_Y} = \frac{P_X}{P_Y}$$

  • Economic Condition: The Marginal Utility per dollar spent on each good must be equal: $$\frac{MU_X}{P_X} = \frac{MU_Y}{P_Y}$$

Example: Utility Maximization

Data:

  • Budget = $40.
  • Price of Burger = $5, Price of Coke = $4.
  • Optimal Solution: 4 Burgers and 5 Cokes.
  • Why? Because at these quantities, the Marginal Utility per dollar is equal (4 utility units per dollar).

Individual Consumer Demand

Derivation:

  • Connects utility-maximizing quantities with different price levels.
  • Find the optimal equilibrium point for each new budget line resulting from a change in Price of X (holding Income and Price of Y constant).
  • The slope of the demand curve shows the Law of Demand (inverse relationship between Price and Quantity).

Market Demand and Marginal Utility

Market Demand:

  • Derived by Horizontally Summing the demand curves of all individuals in the market.
  • Prices along the market demand curve measure the Economic Value of each unit.
  • The demand curve shows the Marginal Benefit (MB) consumers place on the last unit consumed.

Deriving Market Demand (Example)

Table Shows:

  • How to sum quantities demanded by each consumer (1, 2, 3) at each price level to get Total Market Demand.
  • Example: At Price 4, Quantity Demanded = 8 + 3 + 1 = 12.

Substitution and Income Effects

When price changes, the total effect on quantity demanded splits into two parts:

  1. Substitution Effect:
    • Change in consumption due to change in relative prices only (assuming same utility level).
    • Always negative (inverse to price): Price increase reduces quantity demanded (due to switching to cheaper substitutes).
  2. Income Effect:
    • Change in consumption resulting from the change in purchasing power of real income.
    • For Normal Goods: Reinforces substitution effect (Price Drop -> Purchasing Power Up -> Buy More).
    • For Inferior Goods: Opposes substitution effect.

Graphical Analysis of Price Decrease (X)

Figure Shows:

  • Panel A (Normal Good): Income and Substitution effects work in the same direction (Increase Quantity).
  • Panel B (Inferior Good): Income effect works against Substitution effect, but Substitution effect is (usually) stronger, so the relationship remains inverse between Price and Quantity.

Demand Estimation and Forecasting

Direct Demand Estimation Methods

1. Consumer Interviews:

  • Range from talking to shoppers to administering detailed questionnaires.
  • Potential Problems: Selecting a representative sample, and Response Bias (difference between what consumers say and what they do).

2. Market Studies:

  • Trying to hold other factors constant and changing only the price to observe demand.

3. Experiments:

  • Lab Experiments: Using volunteers to simulate shopping conditions.
  • Field Experiments: Observing actual consumer behavior in the market.

Empirical Demand Functions

  • Linear Function Form: $$Q = a + bP + cM + dP_R + eN$$ Where: $Q$ Quantity, $P$ Price, $M$ Income, $P_R$ Price of Related Good, $N$ Number of buyers.

Expected Signs and Elasticities

Expected Signs:

  • b (Price Coeff): Negative (Law of Demand).
  • c (Income Coeff): Positive for Normal Goods, Negative for Inferior Goods.
  • d (Related Good Coeff): Positive for Substitutes, Negative for Complements.

Calculating Elasticities: $$\hat{E} = \hat{b} \frac{P}{Q}, \quad \hat{E}M = \hat{c} \frac{M}{Q}, \quad \hat{E}{XR} = \hat{d} \frac{P_R}{Q}$$

Linear Forecasting

  • Using Regression Analysis to estimate coefficient values.
  • Statistical Significance is determined by comparing t-statistic to critical values or examining the p-value.

Applied Example: Checkers Pizza

Regression Results:

  • R-Square = 0.9555 (Model explains 95.5% of variations).
  • Price Coefficient (P) = -213.422 (Negative as expected, and statistically significant because P-value is very small).
  • Income Coefficient (M) = 0.09109 (Positive -> Normal Good).

Forecasting Quantity: At given prices and income, we forecast Quantity Demanded $Q = 2,784.4$.

Calculating Elasticities for Example:

  • Price Elasticity $\hat{E} = -0.694$ (Inelastic).
  • Income Elasticity $\hat{E}_M = 0.871$ (Necessary Good).
  • Cross-Price Elasticity with Competitor (Al) = 0.368 (Substitute).

Elasticity and Demand

Price Elasticity of Demand (E)

Concept:

  • A measure of how sensitive consumers are to changes in the price of a good.
  • Law of Demand: Ensures the sign is always negative (inverse relationship).
  • Value: Usually expressed as absolute value (positive).

Basic Formula: $$E = \frac{\%\Delta Q}{\%\Delta P}$$

Classification of Elasticity

The higher the absolute value, the more sensitive consumers are.

| Elasticity Type | Value ($|E|$) | Description | | :--- | :--- | :--- | | Elastic | $|E| > 1$ | % Change in Q > % Change in P. | | Unitary Elastic | $|E| = 1$ | % Change in Q = % Change in P. | | Inelastic | $|E| < 1$ | % Change in Q < % Change in P. |

Calculating Price Elasticity

1. Point Elasticity: $$E = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta P} \times \frac{P}{Q}$$

2. Arc Elasticity: Used when calculating elasticity between two points, using average. $$E = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta P} \times \frac{\text{Average } P}{\text{Average } Q}$$

Example Calculation (Arc Elasticity)

Between Points A and B:

  • Point A: P = 20, Q = 14.
  • Point B: P = 26, Q = 10.

  • Calc: $$E = \frac{10 - 14}{(10 + 14)/2} \div \frac{26 - 20}{(26 + 20)/2} = \frac{-4}{12} \div \frac{6}{23} = |-1.27| = 1.27$$

  • Result: Elastic (because 1.27 > 1).

Elasticity along Linear Demand Curve

  • On a straight demand line, Slope is constant but Elasticity changes.
  • Elasticity decreases as we move down the curve (left to right):
    • Top (Price Intercept): $E = \infty$.
    • Top Half: Elastic ($|E| > 1$).
    • Midpoint: Unitary ($|E| = 1$).
    • Bottom Half: Inelastic ($|E| < 1$).
    • Bottom (Quantity Intercept): $E = 0$.

Elasticity and Total Revenue (TR)

Total Revenue ($TR$) = Price $\times$ Quantity. Relationship depends on Elasticity:

Elasticity Type Price Increase Effect on TR Price Decrease Effect on TR Reason
Elastic Decrease Revenue Increase Revenue Quantity effect (Large) dominates Price effect.
Unitary No Change No Change TR is Maximized.
Inelastic Increase Revenue Decrease Revenue Price effect dominates Quantity effect (Small).

Takeaway for Managers:

  • To increase revenue in Elastic region: Lower Price.
  • To increase revenue in Inelastic region: Raise Price.

Numerical Examples

  • Inelastic Demand ($E_D < 1$):
    • Price increase from 1 to 2 leads to Revenue increase from 9 to 16.
  • Elastic Demand ($E_D > 1$):
    • Price increase from 8 to 9 leads to Revenue drop from 16 to 9.

Marginal Revenue (MR)

Definition:

  • Change in Total Revenue from selling one extra unit.
  • Slope of TR curve.
  • Formula: $$MR = \frac{\Delta TR}{\Delta Q}$$

Relationship (Demand, MR, TR):

  • MR > 0: TR Increasing (Elastic).
  • MR = 0: TR Maximized (Unitary).
  • MR < 0: TR Decreasing (Inelastic).
  • MR curve is always below Demand Curve and steeper.

Factors Affecting Price Elasticity

  1. Availability of Substitutes: More substitutes -> Higher elasticity (more sensitive).
  2. Share of Budget: Higher share of budget -> Higher elasticity.
  3. Time Period: Longer time period -> Higher elasticity (consumers find substitutes over time).

Other Elasticities

1. Income Elasticity ($E_M$)

  • Response of Quantity to Change in Income.

  • Formula: $$E_M = \frac{\%\Delta Q}{\%\Delta M}$$

  • Types:

    • Positive (+): Normal Good.
    • Negative (-): Inferior Good.

2. Cross-Price Elasticity ($E_{XY}$)

  • Response of Quantity of X to Change in Price of Y.

  • Formula: $$E_{XY} = \frac{\%\Delta Q_X}{\%\Delta P_Y}$$

  • Types:

    • Positive (+): Substitutes.
    • Negative (-): Complements.

Week 2 Practice Problems (Excel)

Question 1: Budget Constraint

Assume Price of X is \$20 and Price of Y is \$40.

1) Calculate Income

  • Question: If U1 is the highest utility level consumer can reach, what is the income?
  • Answer: Calculate income at endpoints (intercepts).
    • Income = (40 units of X) × (\$20/unit) = \$800.
    • Or Income at point A = (20 units of Y) × (\$40/unit) = \$800.

2) Maximizing Utility Choice

  • Question: How many units of X will consumer choose if Point B is the optimal choice?
  • Solution: Income at Point B is \$1200 (from hypothetical solution data).
    • Equation: $1200 = (\$20 \times X) + (\$40 \times 14)$
    • $1200 = 20X + 560$
    • $640 = 20X$
    • $X = 32$ units.

3) Change in Income

  • Question: If income is \$800, how many units of Y will consumer choose?
  • Solution:
    • At Point A, Income is \$800.
    • Equation: $800 = (\$20 \times 24) + (\$40 \times Y)$
    • $800 = 480 + 40Y$
    • $320 = 40Y$
    • $Y = 8$ units.

Question 2: TR & Elasticity

Answer based on data:

1) Price to sell 100 units

  • Question: What is the price at which 100 units can be sold?
  • Solution:
    • At 100 units, $TR = \$4,500$.
    • $TR = P \times Q \Rightarrow 4500 = P \times 100$
    • $P = \$45$.

2) Maximizing Total Revenue

  • Question: At what price is TR maximized?
  • Solution:
    • On linear demand curve, TR is maximized at the Midpoint.
    • Price = \$25.

3) Marginal Revenue (MR)

  • Question: What is the MR of the 100th unit?
  • Solution: MR is slope of TR. From graph/data = 40.0.

4) Elasticity Type

  • Question: At 700 units, is demand elastic or inelastic?
  • Solution: At 700 units, $MR$ is negative (Bottom half of demand).
    • Demand is Inelastic.

5) Max Total Revenue

  • Question: What is the maximum possible TR?
  • Solution: At midpoint ($P=\$25$, $Q=500$).
    • $TR = 25 \times 500 = \mathbf{\$12,500}$.

Question 3: Elasticity Calculation

Data: | Price | Qd | | :--- | :--- | | 200 | 1000 | | 150 | 1400 | | 100 | 1800 |

1) Arc Elasticity - Case 1

  • Question: If price falls from \$200 to \$150, what is price elasticity?
  • Solution:
    • $E = \frac{\Delta Q / Average Q}{\Delta P / Average P}$
    • $E = \frac{(1400 - 1000) / 1200}{(200 - 150) / 175} = \frac{400/1200}{50/175} \approx \mathbf{1.17}$

2) Classification

  • Question: Elastic or Inelastic?
  • Solution: Since $|E| > 1$, it is Elastic.

3) Marginal Revenue (MR)

  • Question: As output increases from 1000 to 1400, what is MR?
  • Solution:
    • $MR = \frac{\Delta TR}{\Delta Q}$
    • $TR_1 = 200 \times 1000 = 200,000$
    • $TR_2 = 150 \times 1400 = 210,000$
    • $\Delta TR = 10,000$
    • $\Delta Q = 400$
    • $MR = 10,000 / 400 = \mathbf{\$25}$ per unit.

4) Arc Elasticity - Case 2

  • Question: If price falls from \$150 to \$100?
  • Solution:
    • $E = \frac{(1800 - 1400) / 1600}{(150 - 100) / 125} = \frac{400/1600}{50/125} = \mathbf{0.625}$
    • Inelastic ($|E| < 1$).

5) Relationship with TR

  • Question: If price rises from \$100 to \$150, what happens to TR?
  • Solution: In inelastic region, raising price Increases Total Revenue.

Question 4: Demand Estimation

Estimated Function: $$Q = a - bP + cM + dP_R$$

1) Price Coefficient Sign

  • Question: Does P coefficient have expected sign?
  • Solution: Yes. Law of Demand requires negative coefficient.

2) Income Coefficient (M)

  • Question: What does M coefficient tell us?
  • Solution: Positive (+0.32). As Income rises, Q rises. Normal Good.

3) Related Good ($P_R$)

  • Question: Relationship?
  • Solution: Positive coefficient. As competitor price rises, our demand rises. Substitutes.

4) Forecasting (Income Change)

  • Question: If Income decreases by \$1000?
  • Solution: $\Delta Q = \Delta M \times \text{Coeff}_M$
    • $\Delta Q = -1000 \times 0.32 = \mathbf{-320}$ units.

5) Forecasting (Price Change)

  • Question: If Price falls by \$4?
  • Solution: $\Delta Q = \Delta P \times \text{Coeff}_P$
    • Assume coefficient is -5.
    • $\Delta Q = -4 \times (-5) = \mathbf{+20}$ units (Increase).
