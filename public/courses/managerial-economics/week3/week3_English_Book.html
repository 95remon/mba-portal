Production & Cost in the Short Run - Summary

Slide 1: Introduction

Chapter 8: Production and Cost in the Short Run

Slide 2: Production Theory

  • Production Function: Shows the maximum possible output obtainable from a given set of inputs and current technology.
  • Technical Efficiency: Achieved when the maximum amount of output is produced with a given set of inputs.
  • Economic Efficiency: Achieved when the firm produces a given amount of output at the lowest possible total cost.

Slide 3: Inputs

  • Variable Input: Can be easily changed (e.g., labor, raw materials).
  • Fixed Input: Cannot be easily changed in the short run; its cost must be paid even if output is zero (e.g., buildings, equipment).
  • Quasi-Fixed Input: Inputs used in a fixed amount for any positive level of output, but cost is zero if output is zero.

Slide 4: Short Run vs. Long Run

  • Short Run: A period where at least one input is fixed. Output is changed by changing variable inputs only.
  • Long Run: A period where all inputs are variable. Output is changed by changing all inputs.

Slide 5: Production in the Short Run

In the short run, Capital (K) is fixed, and Labor (L) is variable. Changes in output result only from changing the number of workers. * Average Product of Labor (AP): The average output per worker. $$ AP = Q / L $$ * Marginal Product of Labor (MP): The change in total output resulting from adding one unit of labor. $$ MP = \Delta Q / \Delta L $$

Slide 6: Production Table

The table shows the relationship between number of workers, Total Product, Marginal Product, and Average Product. Notice how production changes as the number of workers increases.

Slide 7: Relationship between AP & MP

  • When AP is increasing, MP is greater than AP ($MP > AP$).
  • When AP is decreasing, MP is less than AP ($MP < AP$).
  • When AP is at its maximum, it equals MP ($AP = MP$).

Law of Diminishing Marginal Product: At a certain point, adding more variable inputs (workers) while keeping other inputs fixed leads to a decrease in Marginal Product.

Slide 8: Production Graphs

The graphs show the stages of production: 1. Increasing Marginal Returns: MP is increasing. 2. Diminishing Marginal Returns: MP is decreasing but positive. 3. Negative Marginal Returns: MP becomes negative (Total Product decreases).

Slide 9: Table of Total, Average, and Marginal Products

Detailed numerical table showing calculations with fixed Capital ($K=2$) and varying Labor ($L$).

Slide 10: Curves of Total, Average, and Marginal Products

Graphical representation of the previous table. Shows intersection points and production stages.

Slide 11: Short Run Production Costs

  • Total Variable Cost (TVC): Changes with the level of output (increases as output increases).
  • Total Fixed Cost (TFC): Does not change with the level of output (constant).
  • Total Cost (TC): Sum of fixed and variable costs. $$ TC = TVC + TFC $$

Slide 12: Cost Table in Short Run

Numerical example showing how variable and total costs change with output, while fixed cost remains constant.

Slide 13: Total Cost Curves

Graph of Fixed, Variable, and Total Costs. * TFC curve is a horizontal straight line. * TC curve starts from Fixed Cost (not zero).

Slide 14: Average & Marginal Costs

  • Average Variable Cost (AVC): $AVC = TVC / Q$
  • Average Fixed Cost (AFC): $AFC = TFC / Q$
  • Average Total Cost (ATC): $ATC = TC / Q = AVC + AFC$
  • Marginal Cost (MC): The change in Total Cost from producing one additional unit. $$ MC = \Delta TC / \Delta Q $$

Slide 15: Example of Average & Marginal Costs

Table showing how to calculate average and marginal costs at different output levels.

Slide 16: Cost Curves

Graphical representation of AFC, AVC, ATC, and MC curves.

Slide 17: Cost Curve Relationships

  • AFC: Always decreases as output increases.
  • ATC & AVC: U-shaped. The vertical distance between them represents AFC.
  • MC (Marginal Cost): Also U-shaped.
  • Cuts AVC curve at its minimum point.
  • Cuts ATC curve at its minimum point.
  • When MC is below Average (AVC or ATC), the Average is decreasing.
  • When MC is above Average, the Average is increasing.

Slide 18: Graph of Relationships

Clearly shows the intersection of MC with the minimum points of ATC and AVC.

Slide 19: Relationships between Production and Cost

Inverse relationship between productivity and cost: * When Marginal Product (MP) increases, Marginal Cost (MC) decreases. * When MP decreases, MC increases. * When MP is at its maximum, MC is at its minimum. * Similar relationship applies to AP and AVC.

Slide 20: Summary (Productivity & Cost)

  • Increasing Marginal Productivity $\leftarrow$ Decreasing Marginal Cost.
  • Decreasing Average Productivity $\leftarrow$ Increasing Average Cost. Graph shows how cost curves are the "mirror image" of production curves.

Production & Cost in the Long Run - Summary

Slide 1: Introduction

Chapter 9: Production and Cost in the Long Run

Slide 2: Production in the Long Run

  • Long Run: All inputs (Labor and Capital) are variable.
  • Isoquants: Used to study production decisions in the long run.
  • Goal: Choose the optimal combination of inputs (Labor and Capital) to maximize profit/minimize cost for a given output level.

Slide 3: Production Isoquants

  • Isoquant: A curve showing all possible combinations of inputs (L, K) that produce a specific level of output.
  • Downward Sloping: If we use more labor, we need less capital to produce the same quantity.

Slide 4: Marginal Rate of Technical Substitution (MRTS)

  • MRTS: The rate at which one input is substituted for another while maintaining the same level of output.
  • It is the slope of the Isoquant. $$ MRTS = - \Delta K / \Delta L $$

Slide 5: MRTS (continued)

MRTS can be expressed as the ratio of the Marginal Products of the two inputs: $$ MRTS = MP_L / MP_K $$

Slide 6: Isocost Curves

  • Shows different combinations of inputs that can be purchased for a given total cost.
  • Cost Equation: $C = wL + rK$ (where w: wage rate, r: price of capital).
  • The further the line from the origin, the higher the cost.
  • Changes in input prices change the slope of the line.

Slide 7: Graph of Isocost Curves

Shows how the line shifts with changes in Total Cost or input prices.

Slide 8: Optimal Input Combination

  • Minimum cost for producing a quantity $Q^*$ is achieved at the point of tangency between the Isoquant and the Isocost curve.
  • Optimality Condition: $$ MP_L / w = MP_K / r $$ (The marginal product per dollar spent on each input must be equal).

Slide 9: Graph of Optimal Mix

Shows the tangency point that achieves the lowest possible cost for a given output level.

Slide 10: Expansion Path

  • Expansion Path: A line connecting the points of tangency (optimal mix points) at different levels of output.
  • Shows the least-cost combination for every possible output level, assuming input prices are fixed.
  • Along this path, MRTS equals the ratio of input prices.

Slide 11: Graph of Expansion Path

Shows the expansion path as a curve passing through successive tangency points.

Slide 12: Returns to Scale

If all inputs increase by a certain percentage, how does output change? 1. Increasing Returns to Scale: Output increases by a larger percentage. 2. Decreasing Returns to Scale: Output increases by a smaller percentage. 3. Constant Returns to Scale: Output increases by the same percentage.

Slide 13: Long-Run Average Cost (LAC)

  • Measures unit cost when the optimal quantity of each input is used.
  • LAC curve is U-shaped.
  • Decreasing Segment: Indicates Economies of Scale.
  • Increasing Segment: Indicates Diseconomies of Scale.

Slide 14: Long-Run Marginal Cost (LMC)

  • Measures the change in long-run total cost as output changes.
  • Cuts the LAC curve at its minimum point.
  • When LMC < LAC, LAC decreases.
  • When LMC > LAC, LAC increases.

Slide 15: LMC & LAC Curves

Graph showing the U-shape relationship between the two curves.

Slide 16: Economies of Scale

  • Economies of Scale: Occur when Average Cost (LAC) decreases as output increases.
  • Diseconomies of Scale: Occur when Average Cost (LAC) increases as output increases.

Slide 17: Different Shapes of LAC

LAC shapes vary by industry and production technology.

Slide 18: Economies of Scope

  • Economies of Scope: Occur when the cost of producing two goods (X and Y) together in one firm is less than producing them in two separate firms. $$ C(X, Y) < C(X) + C(Y) $$

Slide 19: Relationship between Short and Long Run Costs

  • LAC curve is the "envelope" of Short-Run Average Cost (SAC) curves.
  • At every output level, the SAC curve is tangent to the LAC curve at one point (the optimal plant size for that output).
  • At the point of tangency, Short-Run Marginal Cost (SMC) equals Long-Run Marginal Cost (LMC).

Week 3 Practice Problems - Summary

Question 1

Given: Fixed Costs (FC) = 20. Complete the table and answer the following questions:

Output Variable Costs
1 10
2 18
3 30
4 45
5 65

Questions: 1. What is Total Cost (TC) at 5 units? What is Average Total Cost (ATC)? 2. What is Marginal Cost (MC) at 5 units? 3. What is the minimum value of ATC? At what output level does this occur?

Answer 1

  1. Total Cost (TC): $$ TC = VC + FC $$ At 5 units, $VC = 65$ and $FC = 20$. $$ TC = 65 + 20 = 85 $$ Average Total Cost (ATC): $$ ATC = TC / Q = 85 / 5 = 17 $$

  2. Marginal Cost (MC): $$ MC = \Delta TC / \Delta Q $$ At 5 units (change from 4 to 5): $$ MC = (85 - 65) / (5 - 4) = 20 $$ (Note: At 4 units, TC = 45 + 20 = 65)

  3. Minimum ATC: From table (calculated):

  4. Q=1: ATC = (10+20)/1 = 30
  5. Q=2: ATC = (18+20)/2 = 19
  6. Q=3: ATC = (30+20)/3 = 16.67
  7. Q=4: ATC = (45+20)/4 = 16.25 (Minimum)
  8. Q=5: ATC = (65+20)/5 = 17 Answer: 16.25 at 4 units.

Question 2

Use the following table to answer the questions below: (Table shows Total Production from different combinations of Labor and Capital - assumed known or refer to original)

Questions: 1. If Capital is fixed at 1 unit, how much does the 3rd worker add to Total Production? 2. If Capital is fixed at 1 unit, at what point do diminishing returns occur? 3. If Capital is fixed at 4 units and there are 2 workers, what is the Average Product of Labor? 4. If Labor is fixed at 3 units, how much does the 2nd unit of Capital add to Total Production?

Answer 2

  1. Marginal Product of 3rd Worker (at K=1): Output at 3 workers = 150. Output at 2 workers = 110. Increase = $150 - 110 = 40$ units.

  2. Point of Diminishing Returns: Calculate MP for each worker (at K=1):

  3. Worker 1: 50
  4. Worker 2: $110 - 50 = 60$ (Increasing)

  5. Worker 3: $150 - 110 = 40$ (Decreasing) Answer: Starts at the 3rd worker.

  6. Average Product of Labor (at K=4, L=2): From table, at 4 Capital and 2 Labor, Output = 390. $$ AP = Q / L = 390 / 2 = 195 $$

  7. Marginal Product of 2nd Unit of Capital (at L=3): Output at 2 units Capital (and 3 Labor) = 360. Output at 1 unit Capital (and 3 Labor) = 150. Increase = $360 - 150 = 210$ units.

Question 3

Assume Labor (the only variable input) has the Average and Marginal Product curves shown below. Price of Labor is $1,000 per unit ($w = 1000$).

Questions: 1. How many workers at minimum AVC? What is the corresponding output? 2. What is the value of minimum AVC? 3. How many workers maximize profit (implied: where MC is essential)? Or rather: How many workers at minimum MC? And corresponding output? 4. What is the value of minimum MC?

Answer 3

  1. Minimum AVC: Occurs at maximum AP. From Graph: Max AP at 450 workers. Output: $Q = AP \times L = 2025 \times 450 = 911,250$ units.

  2. Value of Minimum AVC: $$ AVC = w / AP_{max} = 1000 / 2025 \approx 0.49 \text{ dollars} $$

  3. Minimum MC: Occurs at maximum MP. From Graph: Max MP at 300 workers. Output: $Q = AP \times L = 1800 \times 300 = 540,000$ units. (Using AP at 300 workers which is approx 1800 from graph/data).

  4. Value of Minimum MC: $$ MC = w / MP_{max} = 1000 / 2700 \approx 0.37 \text{ dollars} $$

Question 4

Price of Capital (r) is $20.

Questions: 1. What is the price of Labor (w)? 2. What is the optimal mix to produce 5,000 units at minimum cost? 3. What is the lowest possible cost to produce 5,000 units? 4. What is the lowest possible cost to produce 14,000 units? 5. At the optimal mix for 14,000 units, what is the MRTS?

Answer 4

  1. Price of Labor (w): From graph, the first Isocost line cuts K-axis at 90 and L-axis at 60. Total Cost of first line = $90 \times 20 = 1800$. So $1800 = w \times 60 \Rightarrow w = 1800 / 60 = 30$ dollars.

  2. Optimal Mix for 5,000 units: Tangency point of 5000 unit Isoquant. From Graph: 60 units Capital and 20 units Labor.

  3. Lowest Cost for 5,000 units: This point lies on the first Isocost line calculated earlier. Cost = 1800 dollars.

  4. Lowest Cost for 14,000 units: Tangency point lies on the second Isocost line. This line cuts K-axis at 180. Cost = $180 \times 20 = 3600$ dollars.

  5. MRTS at 14,000 units: MRTS is the slope of the Isoquant (absolute value), which equals the ratio of input prices at equilibrium. $$ MRTS = w / r = 30 / 20 = 1.5 $$
